01
Logo
The favpoll mark combines a half-heart — rotated 45° to suggest both love and direction — with three horizontal bars that echo the product's ranking UI. The icon and wordmark always appear together. Never separate them.
Logo on backgrounds
On white
On light purple
On purple (reversed)
On dark (email headers etc.)
✓ Do

Use the logo at its native size or larger. Always use the supplied SVG file. Maintain clear space equal to the height of the wordmark on all sides. Use on white, light purple, purple, or dark backgrounds only.

✗ Don't

Don't stretch, rotate, recolour, or add effects to the logo. Don't separate the icon from the wordmark. Don't place on photographic backgrounds. Don't recreate the logo in another typeface.

Minimum size

The logo should never appear smaller than 55px wide in digital contexts, or 18mm wide in print. Below this size use the icon mark only (the half-heart + bars element).

04
Voice & tone
favpoll speaks with warmth and quiet dignity. It is never corporate, never salesy, never cute. The tone shifts with the occasion — measured at a memorial, lighter at a birthday — but the character is constant: thoughtful, human, honest.
Tone by occasion
OccasionToneAvoid
Memorial / FuneralQuiet, dignified, unhurried. No upbeat language. No exclamation marks.Cheerful language, "celebrate", "exciting"
BirthdayWarm, personal, can be playful. Light humour welcome.Saccharine, hollow enthusiasm
RetirementRespectful, celebratory, reflective.Corporate language, career clichés
WeddingJoyful, romantic, a little elevated.Overworked wedding tropes
Get well soonWarm, gentle, hopeful. Never presumptuous about recovery.Forced positivity, medical language
General / platformClear, purposeful, human. Acknowledge the full range of occasions without leading with death.Fundraising clichés, tech jargon

05
The framing and reveal
Every favpoll question has two parts: a framing that withholds the protagonist's favourite, and a reveal shown after the guest pledges. The reveal is the emotional heart of the product. Its tone changes completely with the occasion.
Memorial — measured, first person
Question (framing)
"Belinda had a colour she returned to all her life — what's yours?"
After pledging (reveal)
"Mine was purple. I wore it to every occasion that mattered."
Birthday — warm, playful
Question (framing)
"Tell us your favourite flavour ice cream to find out Poppy's."
After pledging (reveal)
"Mint choc chip, obviously. (What do you mean you don't know that already?)"
Retirement — reflective
Question (framing)
"Margaret had a season she always loved most — which is yours?"
After pledging (reveal)
"Autumn. She said it always felt like the world was making itself beautiful one last time."
Rules for framing and reveal copy
RuleWhy
The framing withholdsDon't reveal the protagonist's favourite in the question. The mystery is the mechanic.
The reveal is specificNot "she loved purple" — "Mine was purple. I wore it to every occasion that mattered." Specific details are what people remember.
Memorial reveals are first person where possibleIf the protagonist wrote their own reveal, their voice lives on. Encourage this in the organiser flow.
Birthday reveals can use humourA parenthetical aside, a mock-outrage. The reveal earns the joke because the guest has already pledged.
Never crowbar a topicIf the organiser can't write a natural framing, the topic probably doesn't fit the occasion. The friction is the filter.
